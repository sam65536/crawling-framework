Commonwealth Bank of Australia

CBA accused of 50,000 breaches of money laundering laws

Financial crime-fighting agency alleges Australia’s biggest bank targeted by drug dealers

August 3, 2017

by: Jamie Smyth in Sydney

Listen to this article

00:00
00:00

Australia’s financial crime-fighting agency has accused Commonwealth Bank of Australia, the country’s biggest lender, of more than 50,000 breaches of anti-money laundering and counterterrorism laws.

Court papers filed on Thursday by the Australian Transaction Reports and Analysis Centre (Austrac) allegedly show how deposits using the bank’s intelligent deposit machines (IDMs) were used by drug dealers and other criminals to launder money.

In one case, Austrac alleged that CBA failed to report on time A$5.7m in suspicious transactions in 2014-15 made by three individuals, who have since been charged with drug importation offences. One of the individuals has been convicted.

The anti-money laundering case is the largest yet taken by Austrac, and marks the latest in a series of financial scandals that have focused a spotlight on alleged rule breaking by Australian banks.

Austrac has accused CBA of more than 50,000 breaches of the law by failing to adequately monitor A$624.7m of transactions on its network of IDMs over a three-year period. The agency also alleged that CBA failed to adequately report unusual transactions totalling more than A$77m, or monitor suspicious customers after it became aware of possible money laundering.

Peter Clark, Austrac’s acting chief executive, said the action should send a message to financial institutions about their legal obligations. 

“By failing to have sound anti-money laundering and counterterrorism financing systems and controls in place, businesses are at risk of being misused for criminal purposes,” he said.

The alleged breaches relate to CBA’s rollout in May 2012 of IDMs that facilitate anonymous cash and cheque deposits of up to A$20,000 per transaction. The machines count the cash and instantly credit the nominated recipient account.

Austrac alleged that the machines can be used to facilitate payment for illegal dealings, with criminals able to accept cash from anonymous parties and then transfer the proceeds of crime overseas.

CBA allegedly failed to carry out an anti-money laundering risk assessment before the rollout nor did it review its risk assessment in response to concerns raised by law enforcement about significant instances of money laundering through IDMs, according to court documents filed by Austrac. 

The bank is required to report any transaction above A$10,000 to Austrac within 10 working days. The agency alleged that CBA failed to provide 53,506 threshold transaction reports on time.

CBA said it had been in discussions with Austrac and had co-operated with its requests.

“We take our regulatory obligations extremely seriously and we are one of the largest reporters to Austrac. On an annual basis we report over 4m transactions to Austrac in an effort to identify and combat any suspicious activity as quickly and efficiently as we can,” the bank said.

“We are reviewing the nature of the proceedings and will have more to say on the specific claims in due course,” CBA added.

CBA could face a maximum penalty of A$18m for each breach of the law.
